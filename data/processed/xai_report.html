Why network embeddings cluster interactors

Network embeddings (e.g. STRING's DeepWalk / node2vec-style vectors) encode topological proximity in the known PPI graph. Proteins that share many interaction partners end up in nearby regions of the embedding space — which is exactly the signal a classifier needs. Sequence embeddings, by contrast, capture evolutionary / structural similarity; two proteins can be structurally unrelated yet functionally coupled (e.g. scaffold proteins binding diverse partners), so their sequence vectors will appear far apart even though they interact.

3 · Feature-Component Ablation answers Q2

Network embedding pairs are encoded as two components: |e₁ − e₂| (difference) captures how dissimilar two proteins are in embedding space; e₁ ⊙ e₂ (element-wise product) captures shared directions — i.e. co-membership in functional modules.

Biological interpretation

  • ∥ |e₁ − e₂| dominates → the model distinguishes interactors by how different their network positions are. This encodes complementarity: e.g. a kinase and its substrate occupy distinct network niches yet interact.
  • ⊙ e₁ ⊙ e₂ contributes → shared embedding directions reflect co-complex membership (same protein complex → same neighbours → similar embedding dimensions are large simultaneously).

7 · What the Model Learned About Protein Biology answers Q2 + Q3

Chemistry & biology encoded in network embeddings

  • 1 Functional modules / pathways: STRING edge weights aggregate co-expression, co-localisation, and experimental evidence. Node2vec-style walks therefore encode pathway membership; proteins in the same KEGG pathway cluster together.
  • 2 Protein complexes: Dense cliques in the interaction graph become tight clusters in embedding space. The model implicitly learns complex membership without being told the complex identities.
  • 3 Hub vs peripheral proteins: High-degree hubs (e.g. TP53, EGFR) have diffuse embeddings covering many dimensions; peripheral proteins have sparse, specialised vectors. The classifier learns to use this degree-encoded signature.
  • 4 Evolutionary conservation: Orthologs tend to occupy similar embedding positions because their interaction partners are conserved across species — the model picks up cross-species conserved interaction logic.

Additional biological questions answerable with this framework

  • → Novel interaction discovery: Score all unobserved pairs with the trained model; top-ranking pairs are candidate novel PPIs for experimental validation (yeast two-hybrid, co-IP).
  • → Drug target identification: Proteins whose embedding neighbourhood is perturbed by a disease mutation (e.g. cancer driver) and yet are predicted to interact with known drug targets are prioritised candidates.
  • → Pathway rewiring in disease: Compare embedding cosine-similarity distributions for the same protein pairs across a healthy vs disease STRING network to identify rewired modules.
  • → Protein function annotation: Use kNN in embedding space to transfer GO-term annotations from characterised to uncharacterised proteins (function-by-neighbourhood).
  • → Tissue-specific interactomes: Retrain with tissue-specific co-expression data (GTEx) as STRING channel; use SHAP to find which tissue contexts drive interaction probability.
  • → Cross-species interaction transfer: Map human network embeddings to model-organism embeddings via alignment; predict conserved interactions in poorly-studied organisms.
  • → PPI-disrupting variant prioritisation: Encode wild-type and mutant sequence, compute how much the mutant's embedding shifts, feed into the interaction model to flag variants that likely disrupt key interactions.

Limitations & caveats

  • ! Network embeddings are circular: STRING's combined_score is itself derived from computational predictions. High accuracy may partly reflect learning the STRING scoring function rather than true biology.
  • ! Negative sampling is uncertain: medium-confidence pairs used as negatives may contain real interactions not yet characterised.
  • ! Protein-wise split is the correct evaluation but the held-out proteins still share functional categories with training proteins — true zero-shot generalisation to entirely new protein families is likely worse.
